Linus rolled back out onto the gutter system in a fit of rage. He considered going to Kringle or an otherwise close friend, but such a thought was fleeting, as Kringle, nor anyone else, was his “close friend”. He spun about in his glass orb, rolling about the building. He came up to a hamster, who seemed to hold no special qualities whatsoever (aside from his ability to talk). The hamster turned to him, saying, “'Sup.”

Linus scowled at the furry little creature, and imagined how if he had a body he would crush all of his kind in his left hand.

“How's it goin'?” the hamster asked.

“Why am I even talking to you?”

“Beats me. I'm Louis.”

“Like I really care.”

“Yes sir. Louis the Hamster. Pleasure to meet you.”

“Forgive me if I fail to reciprocate the feeling.”

“Not a problem, sir, not a problem at all. What brings you here today?”

“I don't have a body, damn it! Isn't it rather obvious?! And because I haven't a body, I'm rolling around in a ball and talking to hamster, god of thunder help me!”

“Well, there isn't any shame in talking to a hamster, sir, if you don't mind my saying so, nor in not having a body. But I was specifically referring to being here, at the stock exchange, not in the gutter system.”

“What does it look like, you dolt? I'm a trader. We have tools like you to run around on the information exchange system.”

“I would have well expected both sir. Simply making conversation.”

“You're a poor excuse for a creature, you know that?”

Louis looked rather hurt by this comment. “Sir, my profession is just as important as yours, if not more so.”

“Running around the stock exchange floor is more important then making the money funding your existence? Yeah right.”

The hamster nodded and looked away, as if he suddenly remembered the position that society had carved out for him.

“Why do we even use you anyway.” Linus wondered aloud.

“Ah, that, sir, is a long and complicated question. I could explain it, but it would require my binding you to secrecy, and my taking you to a secret place, away from the atrium.”

“Why would I listen to the explanation of a hamster?”

“Sir, we are not emotionless, unthinking creatures. We have a society. We socialize. We group together. We talk, and think, and work.”

“You live a privileged existence.”

“Indeed we do.”

There was silence for a moment.

“Would you care to visit?” asked the hamster.

“Visit what?”

“My home.”

Linus thought.
